THE FOUNDING VISION

On September 13, 2025, at 5:34 AM, Dr. Jalees Razavi created a WhatsApp group that would grow into something neither he nor the initial members quite anticipated. What started as a handful of occupational medicine colleagues connecting after a virtual meeting has evolved into a global movement spanning six continents, with voices from Jakarta to Dublin, S√£o Paulo to Riyadh.

"I started this initiative because I felt our field needed a space for honest, unfiltered conversation ‚Äî a place where Occupational Medicine professionals could speak openly about what truly matters."

Dr. Razavi's frustration was palpable from the start. He named it #OccupationalMedicineUnfiltered‚Äîa deliberate choice.

The early days were energetic. Cameron Kiani joined almost immediately, bringing AI expertise and editing skills to the table. Within hours, colleagues from Indonesia, the UK, Peru, and beyond were introducing themselves. Dr. Mierna Reismala, an Indonesian professor. Norvil Mera Chu, ICOH National Secretary for Peru. CHAPTER 02

A MOVEMENT TAKES SHAPE

By late September, the group had established its rhythm. Dr. Razavi began posting prolifically on LinkedIn‚Äîsometimes three or four posts per day‚Äîeach tagged with #OccupationalMedicineUnfiltered. The pattern became familiar: he'd share a post in the WhatsApp group, then politely but firmly request: "Please like, comment, and repost to amplify our message."

Some members embraced this immediately. Dr. Sujil Jacob from Ireland went back and updated over 80 of his LinkedIn posts with the hashtag. Others contributed by sharing webinars, conferences, and resources. Norvil organized multiple ICOH Peru events on nanotechnology and occupational medicine history. The Credentialing Crisis

A pattern emerged across countries: the erosion of specialist standards in occupational medicine. GPs and other specialists functioning in OH roles without proper training. Hospital employee health clinics not run by certified OEM specialists. Private providers using non-qualified doctors for cost reasons.

  • Turkey: Specialists viewed as "troublemakers" by employers
  • Ireland: No clear distinction between fellowship levels
  • UK: Any doctor can practice as long as "supervised"

Psychological Safety Campaign

Dr. Sheila pushed back on the profession's complacency: "We are so passive as a profession that those working at government level never think of getting experts from the field of OH to lead on workplace health at policy level."

The group began coalescing around the principle that psychological hazards must have regulatory parity with physical and chemical hazards.

The Independence Question

Dr. Razavi shared his belief that there should be "no organizational barrier between a company's Chief Medical Officer and its Chief Executive Officer." Colleagues described pressure to modify clinical opinions to satisfy employers.

CHAPTER 03

THE WELLNESS CRITIQUE

In mid-November, Dr. Razavi published posts that crystallized the group's growing frustration with superficial approaches to worker health. The critique: employers invest in wellness programs‚Äîmeditation apps, yoga classes, fruit bowls‚Äîwhile ignoring fundamental occupational health infrastructure.

"Many employers may sometimes place emphasis on wellness initiatives before fully establishing the essential foundations of access to occupational health."
‚Äî DR. SUJIL JACOB

The group's emerging consensus: wellness is window dressing when workers lack access to proper occupational medicine services, when psychosocial hazards go unaddressed, when non-specialists make high-risk fitness-for-duty decisions.

CHAPTER 04

BUILDING TOWARD ACTION

As November progressed, the group began shifting from diagnosis to prescription. What could be done?

Dr. Razavi started drafting an agenda for a second global Zoom meeting. Topics would include:

Proposed Action Items

  • Global credentialing standards and title protection
  • Structural independence of occupational medicine
  • Regulatory frameworks for psychological safety
  • International workforce planning using WISN models
  • Delphi study leveraging group's global expertise
  • Coordinated publication series in JOEM/Yellow Journal

Dr. Sujil Jacob proposed: "A Delphi study of 73 experts (this WhatsApp membership) will give valuable insights into the specialty."

Dr. Razavi embraced the idea, envisioning not one study but "a coordinated series of studies that will benefit the mission and vision behind #OccupationalMedicineUnfiltered and could be jointly published in The Yellow Journal or JOEM."

EPILOGUE

THE UNFINISHED BUSINESS

As the quarter closed, the group had diagnosed many problems but solved few. The energy remained high, but the path forward remained unclear.

Could a WhatsApp group of 100+ people actually influence regulatory bodies in dozens of countries? Could LinkedIn posts, however viral, create structural change in how occupational medicine is practiced and valued?

"Real change always starts at the grassroots level. It starts with conversations like this one‚Äîunfiltered, honest, and grounded in lived experience."
‚Äî DR. JALEES RAZAVI

But Dr. Anil Adisesh's earlier skepticism lingered: "Good luck to us in effecting change without coming up against small or big 'p' politics."

The quarter ended not with answers, but with momentum. A global community had formed. Uncomfortable truths had been spoken. Potential research collaborations had been identified. And the hashtag #OccupationalMedicineUnfiltered had begun appearing on LinkedIn posts from Jakarta to Cape Town, Dublin to Mumbai.

What Comes Next

The group now faces its first real test: converting conversation into action. Can the energy of these three months translate into actual research output, regulatory advocacy, or changes in professional standards?

The second global Zoom meeting will be telling. The proposed Delphi study and publication series could give the movement academic credibility. The ongoing LinkedIn campaign continues to raise visibility.

The first three months established that occupational medicine professionals worldwide share remarkably similar frustrations. The next three months will test whether they can build something better together.
